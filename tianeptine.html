Tianeptine: What Parents Should Know

Sold as “ZaZa,” “Neptune’s Fix,” or “Pegasus.” It’s not a vitamin — it’s an opioid-acting drug in disguise.

Acts at the μ-opioid receptor. Lab studies confirm tianeptine is a MOR agonist — the same receptor targeted by oxycodone and heroin.1–2

Emergency spikes. Poison centers and CDC reports describe rising U.S. cases: coma, seizures, and respiratory depression — often reversed with naloxone.3–5

Dependence & withdrawal. Daily users develop opioid-type tolerance and withdrawal (anxiety, tremor, GI upset, insomnia). Cases respond to buprenorphine-naloxone therapy.6–7

Not an approved medicine in the U.S. FDA warns consumers not to use tianeptine products marketed as supplements; several states have banned sales.8–10

Parent Playbook

What to watch for. Boxes or blister packs labeled ZaZa, Neptune’s Fix, Pegasus; “mood booster,” “nootropic,” or “energy” claims. Symptoms: sedation, slowed breathing, pinpoint pupils, or agitation and tremor during withdrawal.3–5

How kids get it. Gas stations, smoke shops, and online stores sell it as a “supplement.” Age checks are inconsistent.

If someone is in trouble. Call 911 for severe sleepiness, slowed breathing, bluish lips, or collapse. If you have naloxone, use it. For advice, call Poison Control: 1-800-222-1222.

Talk about it—script. “Some gas-station products are actually opioid-like drugs. Tianeptine is one of them. I’m here to help, not judge. Let’s look at real medical info and make a plan together.”

Myths vs. Facts

Myth: “It’s an antidepressant, so it’s safe.”
Fact: In the U.S., tianeptine is not approved as a medication; misuse and dependence have led to state bans and multiple FDA warnings.8–10

Myth: “It’s just a ‘nootropic.’”
Fact: It is a μ-opioid receptor agonist — risks mirror other opioids (overdose, addiction, withdrawal).1–2
