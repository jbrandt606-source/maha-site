Federal Government

FDA — Food & Drug Administration

What FDA can do
• Test suspect “supplements” and publish warnings
• Send warning letters; seize misbranded/adulterated products
• Coordinate with DEA/DOJ; recommend national scheduling

Where FDA is limited
• Can’t make a drug illegal on its own (that’s DEA)
• Can’t police every gas station / website

Ask FDA to
• Keep testing mood/energy “supplements”
• Post parent-friendly alerts on FDA.gov
• Formally recommend scheduling of kratom, tianeptine, phenibut, etc.

DEA — Drug Enforcement Administration

What DEA can do
• Place substances into Schedule I (ban sales/possession)
• Investigate interstate shippers & distributors

Where DEA is limited
• Scheduling process takes time & evidence
• Industry lobbying can slow action

Ask DEA to
• Use emergency scheduling where justified (e.g., tianeptine)
• Coordinate with states to track online/parcel shipments

U.S. Senators & Representatives

What Congress can do
• Pass national bans & close supplement loopholes
• Hold hearings that press FDA/DEA to move faster
• Fund education, data systems, and enforcement

Where Congress is limited
• Laws take time and votes

Ask Congress to
• Support bans on gas-station drugs (kratom, tianeptine, phenibut, “mushroom gummies”)
• Fund poison-control, hospital surveillance, and school education

State Government

State Senate, House & Governor

What they can do
• Pass statewide bans; restrict sales & marketing
• Fund poison centers & public-health outreach
• Grant fast-track authority to health/pharmacy boards

Limitations
• State law stops at the border; online/interstate sales persist

Ask them to
• Enact full bans and close loopholes
• Resource enforcement and school education programs

State Health Department

What they can do
• Issue health alerts; publish ER/poisoning data
• Temporarily restrict sales under public-health powers

Limitations
• They warn & recommend; they don’t prosecute

Ask them to
• Release frequent alerts; share dashboards
• Partner with schools & local boards for parent education

State Board of Pharmacy

What they can do
• Add substances to the state controlled list
• Stop licensed pharmacies from selling risky products

Limitations
• Limited reach over unlicensed retailers (vape/gas shops)

Ask them to
• Use rule-making to schedule now
• Recommend full legislative bans for non-pharmacy retailers

State Medical Associations

What they can do
• Educate physicians and the public; publish guidance
• Testify & support bans with patient data

Limitations
• Influence policy; don’t enforce it

Ask them to
• Publish position statements on kratom/tianeptine
• Provide testimony and de-identified case summaries

Local Government

City & County Councils

What they can do
• Ban sales inside city limits; require warning signs
• Revoke business licenses; hold public hearings

Limitations
• State law can preempt local rules; enforcement capacity varies

Ask them to
• Pass local bans/resolutions urging state action
• Require signage & age-checks; support compliance checks

Police & Local Law Enforcement

What they can do
• Enforce state/federal drug laws; inspect shops
• Work with health departments on education & evidence collection

Limitations
• Can act only when products are illegal or misbranded

Ask them to
• Map retailers selling these products; support community meetings
• Enforce once bans/ordinances pass

Teachers & Schools

What they can do
• Teach what these “herbal highs” really are
• Watch for new products among teens; share health alerts

Limitations
• Can’t control stores — only education & campus policies

Ask them to
• Add gas-station drugs to health class
• Send flyers to parents; join local prevention coalitions

About MAHA • Mothers Against Herbal Abuse

“Are we supposed to make America healthy again or make America high again?
Hard to say when every other block looks like a carnival for chemical dependency — neon Kratom signs glowing brighter than the pharmacy across the street.”

MAHA began with that question—and the disbelief that our neighborhoods now have more stores selling so-called “wellness highs” than selling actual groceries. Behind the neon, there’s a revolving door of overdoses, ER visits, and untested substances hiding behind the word herbal.

We are citizens who believe public health should come before profit and pseudo-science. We speak out where regulation has failed—against gas-station opioids, mislabeled “herbal” drinks, and online supplement scams that prey on young people.

Our mission is simple:

  • Protect families from unregulated psychoactive products disguised as supplements.
  • Educate lawmakers with real data—not industry marketing myths.
  • Empower communities to demand responsible policy and honest labeling.

Because every “natural high” sold under fluorescent lights chips away at the meaning of health.
Together, we choose to make America healthy again—not high again.
