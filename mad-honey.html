Mad Honey: What Parents Should Know

Marketed online as a “natural remedy” or “energy booster,” mad honey contains grayanotoxins that can trigger dangerous heart rhythms and fainting—even from a single spoonful.

It’s real—and toxic. Bees collecting rhododendron nectar produce honey with grayanotoxins, which disrupt the heart’s electrical system and nerve signaling.1–3

Small amounts can poison. Dizziness, nausea, vomiting, sweating, blurred vision, slow heart rate, low blood pressure, and collapse can occur within minutes to hours.2–3

Hospital cases are documented in the U.S. Travelers and online purchasers have required ED care and monitoring after ingesting imported mad honey (Turkey/Nepal).1–3,5

No proven benefits. Claims for libido, energy, or blood pressure are unsubstantiated; verified effects are toxicity and cardiac complications.1–3

Parent Playbook

What to look for. Honey labeled “mad,” “grayanotoxin,” or imported from the Black Sea region (Turkey) or Nepal; glossy red/brown jars marketed for “performance,” “energy,” or “natural Viagra.”1–3,5

If poisoning is suspected. Call 911 or go to the ER for dizziness, vomiting, slow pulse, fainting, or confusion. Tell clinicians it may be mad honey; bring the container if available.2–3,5

Prevention. Do not purchase “mad honey” online. Remind teens: “natural” does not mean safe — this can slow the heart and cause collapse.1–3

Talk about it — script. “Some imported honey has toxins that can cause fainting or heart problems. Let’s avoid risky products and check official health guidance together.”

Myths vs. Facts

Myth: “It’s an ancient medicine, so it’s safe.”
Fact: Historical use ≠ safety. Modern case series show bradycardia, hypotension, and hospitalizations after ingestion.1–3

Myth: “A small taste can’t hurt.”
Fact: Potency varies widely; even a teaspoon has caused fainting and dangerous arrhythmias.2–3
