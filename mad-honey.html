Mad Honey: What Parents Should Know

Marketed online as a “natural remedy” or “energy booster,” mad honey contains toxins that can trigger dangerous heart rhythms and fainting even from a single spoonful.

It’s real — and toxic. Mad honey is produced by bees that collect nectar from rhododendron flowers. It contains grayanotoxins that disrupt the heart’s electrical system.1–3

Small doses can cause poisoning. Symptoms: dizziness, nausea, vomiting, sweating, blurred vision, dangerously low heart rate, and collapse.2–3

Hospital cases in the U.S. are increasing. Poison centers report travelers and online buyers hospitalized after ingesting imported mad honey from Turkey and Nepal.4–6

No proven benefits. Claims that it improves libido or blood pressure are unsubstantiated. The only verified effects are toxicity and cardiac complications.2–3,5

Parent Playbook

What to look for. Honey labeled “mad,” “grayanotoxin,” or imported from the Black Sea region of Turkey or Nepal; bright red/brown jars marketed for “performance,” “energy,” or “natural Viagra.”4–6

Signs of poisoning. Dizziness, sweating, nausea, vomiting, low pulse, confusion, or fainting minutes to hours after ingestion — call 911 or go to the ER. Tell responders it could be mad honey.2–3,5

How to prevent access. Do not buy “mad honey” online or from ethnic food markets. Warn teens that “natural” doesn’t mean safe — this can stop the heart.

Talk about it — script. “Some imported honey can be toxic and cause heart problems or fainting. Let’s check ingredients and avoid anything that promises ‘energy’ or ‘performance.’”

Myths vs. Facts

Myth: “It’s an ancient medicine.”
Fact: Ancient use doesn’t equal safety. Modern case reports show severe bradycardia, hypotension, and hospitalizations.1–3,5

Myth: “A little bit can’t hurt.”
Fact: Potency varies widely; even a teaspoon has caused fainting and dangerous arrhythmias.2–3
