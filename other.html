Other Common Substances: What Parents Should Know

New “energy,” “focus,” and “mood booster” products in gas stations and vape shops are often marketed as “herbal” or “natural,” but may contain potent synthetic stimulants or other drug-acting compounds. U.S. and international authorities have warned about unsafe ingredients (e.g., DMAA/DMHA), mislabeled imports, and products that hide undisclosed actives or excessive caffeine.1–3 At the same time, opioid-acting ingredients (e.g., 7-OH from kratom) and high-potency hemp derivatives have generated poison-center alerts and emergency department visits — including in children — when sold as “legal alternatives.”4–9 Some “vitality” resins (e.g., Shilajit) have also been found to contain heavy metals when not properly purified.10

Labels are unreliable. “Plant-based” drinks, shots, and gummies may include potent stimulants, opioid-acting alkaloids, or high-THC hemp derivatives — sometimes with inaccurate strength or undisclosed ingredients.1–3,5–7

Real emergencies. Poison-center and hospital surveillance document intoxication, seizures, extreme sedation, pediatric exposures, and withdrawal linked to these products.4,6–9

Official warnings exist. FDA consumer updates and warning letters, CDC alerts, and state health notices address unsafe or mislabeled “herbal” and hemp-derived items.1–2,6–7

“Store shelf” ≠ safety. Being sold in gas/vape shops or online does not guarantee testing, age checks, or accurate ingredient disclosure.1–3,6

Parent Playbook

What to watch for. “Relax/Focus” shots, kava drinks, kratom or chocolate “wellness” bars, hemp/Δ8 gummies, and bright candy-style packs or powder tubs. Warning signs after use: unusual sedation or agitation, vomiting, fast/slow heart rate, confusion, paranoia, or withdrawal symptoms.1–2,4–9

If someone is in trouble. Call 911 for severe sleepiness, breathing problems, seizures, chest pain, or dangerous behavior. Bring the package to the ER. For guidance, call Poison Control: 1-800-222-1222 (U.S.).4,8–9

How kids get it. Vape/gas shops, online “supplement” stores, and social media. “Natural” branding and cartoonish packaging are common; age checks may be weak.1–2,6

Talk about it — script. “If something promises focus, calm, or ‘natural energy,’ that’s a red flag. Labels can hide strong drugs. I want you safe — let’s check trusted sources together.”

Myths vs. Facts

Myth: “If it’s sold over the counter, it must be safe.”
Fact: Many products bypass drug standards; agencies regularly issue warnings and recalls.1–2,6

Myth: “Natural = harmless.”
Fact: “Plant-based” doesn’t prevent overdose, dependence, or interactions; poison-center data show real harm.4,8–9
