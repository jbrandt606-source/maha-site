Other Common Substances: What Parents Should Know

Gas-station/online “herbal highs” (kava drinks, focus or relaxation shots, kratom blends) often hide psychoactive drugs and use deceptive labels. FDA/CDC and state health departments warn of seizures, dependence, and hospitalizations from these unregulated products.

Labels are unreliable. “Plant-based” drinks, shots, and gummies may include potent psychoactives (kratom alkaloids, phenibut/tianeptine analogs, Δ8 THC) with inaccurate strength or undisclosed ingredients.1–2,5

Real emergencies. Poison-center and ED surveillance document intoxication, seizures, extreme sedation, and withdrawal from these products — especially among teens and young adults.2,4

Official warnings exist. FDA issues consumer updates and warning letters; CDC’s Health Alert Network and state health departments post advisories for products like tianeptine and Δ8 THC.1–3,6

“Hemp shop” ≠ safety. Selling in vape/gas shops or online doesn’t guarantee testing or age controls; many products bypass normal drug regulations.1–2,5–6

Parent Playbook

What to watch for. “Relax” or “Focus” shots, “kava drinks,” kratom/chocolate “wellness” bars, Δ8/“hemp” gummies, bright candy-style packs, or powder tubs. Signs: unusual sedation or agitation, vomiting, fast/slow heart rate, paranoia, or withdrawal symptoms.1–2,4–6

If someone is in trouble. Call 911 for severe sleepiness, breathing problems, seizures, chest pain, or dangerous behavior. Keep the product/container for clinicians. For guidance, call Poison Control: 1-800-222-1222 (U.S.).2

How kids get it. Vape/gas shops, online “supplement” stores, and social media. “Natural” branding and cartoon packaging are common; age checks can be weak.1–2,5

Talk about it — script. “If a product promises focus, calm, or ‘natural energy,’ that’s a red flag. Labels may hide strong drugs. I want you safe — let’s check trusted sources together.”

Myths vs. Facts

Myth: “If it’s sold over the counter, it must be safe.”
Fact: Many products bypass drug standards; FDA and states regularly issue warnings and recalls for safety violations.1–3

Myth: “Natural = harmless.”
Fact: “Plant-based” doesn’t prevent overdose, dependence, or drug interactions; poison-center data show real harm.2,4
