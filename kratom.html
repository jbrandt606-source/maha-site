Kratom: What Parents Should Know

Mitragynine converts into 7‑hydroxymitragynine (7‑OH), which binds the same opioid receptors as morphine. Human studies confirm measurable 7‑OH after kratom use; animal work shows 7‑OH can be several times stronger than morphine.

CDC reports hundreds of kratom‑positive overdose deaths across many states, with medical examiners often judging kratom causal. U.S. poison centers recorded 10,272 kratom cases (2014–2023), and clinicians document dependence, withdrawal, and neonatal abstinence.

Kratom: What Parents Should Know

Fast facts for families and community leaders — in plain language, with sources.

Acts like an opioid. Mitragynine turns into 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH) in the body — a compound that binds the same μ-opioid receptor as morphine and can be several times stronger in lab tests.1–4

Real harm signals. CDC identified hundreds of kratom-positive overdose deaths; U.S. poison centers logged 10,272 exposures (2014–2023).5–7

Dependence & withdrawal. Doctors report opioid-type withdrawal and neonatal abstinence after maternal use; some patients require buprenorphine therapy.—

Gas-station access. Sold next to vapes and delta-8; FDA warns no kratom products are approved or proven safe.
