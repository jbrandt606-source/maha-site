Kratom: What Parents Should Know

Quick facts in plain language, with reliable sources you can share.

It acts like an opioid. After swallowing kratom, the body turns mitragynine into 7-hydroxymitragynine (7-OH), which binds the same μ-opioid receptor as morphine.1–3 Animal work shows 7-OH can be several-fold more potent than morphine in pain tests.4–5

Real harm signals. CDC identified hundreds of kratom-positive overdose deaths across many states; medical examiners often judged kratom causal.6–7 U.S. poison centers recorded 10,272 kratom exposures (2014–2023).8

Dependence & withdrawal occur. Doctors report opioid-type withdrawal and newborn withdrawal after maternal use; many cases respond to buprenorphine-naloxone treatment.9–13

Sold like a “supplement,” not a medicine. FDA states no kratom product is approved or proven safe and warns of addiction, withdrawal, seizures, and death.14

Parent Playbook

What to watch for. New “tea” powders/capsules; small tubs or bright sachets; “extract shots;” labels like Maeng Da or “OPMS.” Signs: pinpoint or very dilated pupils, unusual sedation or agitation, nausea, sweating, fast heart rate, or withdrawal (restlessness, body aches, insomnia, GI upset).8,14

How kids get it. Vape/gas shops, online vendors, social media “wellness” marketing, or older friends. Age checks are often weak or absent in online sales.8,14

What to do now. 1) Remove access; 2) Talk to your child (sample script below); 3) Call your pediatrician or family doctor; 4) If severe sleepiness, slowed breathing, seizure, or collapse—call 911 and consider naloxone if available; 5) For guidance, call Poison Control: 1-800-222-1222 (U.S.).8,14

Talk about it—sample script. “Some ‘herbal’ products act like opioids. Kratom turns into a stronger opioid in the body. I’m not mad; I want you safe. Let’s look at what doctors and the FDA actually report and make a plan together.”

Myths vs. Facts

Myth: “It’s just a plant.”
Fact: Plants can contain potent drugs. Kratom’s 7-OH metabolite is an opioid-receptor agonist; lab potency can exceed morphine.1,4–5

Myth: “It’s safer because it’s legal in some places.”
Fact: FDA does not recognize any kratom product as safe or effective; national surveillance shows hospitalizations and deaths.6–8,14
